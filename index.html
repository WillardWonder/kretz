AI News Break · Customer-facing AI

Air Canada chatbot gave wrong fare guidance.

A tribunal found Air Canada liable after a chatbot gave incorrect bereavement fare information to a customer.

Lesson: companies own what their AI tells customers.
Open source story
AI News Break · Fake citations

Lawyers submitted AI-generated fake cases.

In Mata v. Avianca, attorneys were sanctioned after filing legal work containing fabricated cases from ChatGPT.

Lesson: confident output still requires verification.
Open court order
AI News Break · Data exposure

Samsung restricted AI after sensitive code exposure.

Reports said Samsung limited employee use of generative AI tools after sensitive internal information was entered into ChatGPT.

Lesson: employees need clear data rules before adoption spreads.
Open source story
AI News Break · Brand risk

DPD chatbot swore and criticized the company.

DPD disabled part of its AI chatbot after a customer prompted it into inappropriate and brand-damaging responses.

Lesson: public AI needs testing, monitoring, and fallback plans.
Open source story
AI News Break · Publishing errors

CNET’s AI articles needed major corrections.

CNET published AI-generated personal finance articles that later drew corrections and criticism from staff.

Lesson: AI-written content needs editorial review and accountability.
Open source story
AI News Break · Hiring discrimination

iTutorGroup settled an AI hiring discrimination case.

The EEOC said the company’s software rejected qualified applicants based on age and announced a $365,000 settlement.

Lesson: automated decisions can create legal and fairness risk.
Open EEOC source
AI News Break · Prediction failure

Zillow’s algorithmic home-buying model failed.

Zillow shut down its home-flipping operation after its pricing and forecasting model struggled with real-world volatility.

Lesson: prediction tools can fail when conditions change.
Open source story
AI News Break · Operations pilot

McDonald’s ended an AI drive-thru test.

McDonald’s ended its IBM automated drive-thru ordering test after a multi-year pilot at more than 100 restaurants.

Lesson: pilots must survive real customers, noise, edge cases, and operations.

Kretz AI Decision Guide

A governance-first decision tool. Users describe a problem, then the GPT helps decide whether it should be AI, existing software, automation, a narrow GPT, process cleanup, vendor system, or no AI for now.

Open GPTUse in activity

Hands-on activity: try the tools, then decide what to build

  1. Start with the Kretz AI Decision Guide. Describe a real Kretz problem or workflow idea.
  2. Answer its questions and see whether it recommends existing software, automation, a Custom GPT, process cleanup, a vendor system, or no AI for now.
  3. If the problem is communication-related, try the Kretz DNA Assistant with a rough email or message.
  4. Discuss what data the tool needs, what it should never do, who owns it, and what human review is required.

Access note: participants can use shared GPTs if they are signed in and have access to the GPT. Creating or editing GPTs requires a paid subscription and is done in the web experience. If someone cannot access a GPT during the session, they can still complete the activity by pasting the short instructions into a normal ChatGPT conversation.

Responsible use, in practice.

The habits and safeguards that keep AI-assisted work accurate, safe, and trustworthy.

Refine AI policyClarify allowed uses, protected data, approved tools, and review requirements.
Select secure toolsEvaluate ChatGPT Team, Copilot, and other platforms based on usability, controls, cost, and longevity.
Create the Sign-Off RuleNo external, customer-facing, financial, safety, or technical AI-assisted content goes out without human review.
Use No-Fault ReportingMake it easy to report hallucinations, errors, and tool problems so processes improve.
Define Kretz DNACapture voice, tone, values, vocabulary, and owner-thinker culture for AI-assisted work.
Name internal ownersDecide who owns each tool, who handles IT and security questions, and who reviews AI-assisted work before it goes out.
Pilot useful workflowsStart with lower-risk use cases in communication, training, routing, land, sales, and forestry.
Stay adaptableReview tools, policies, and workflows regularly as the AI technology landscape changes.
