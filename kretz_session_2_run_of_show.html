00:0810 min

Bowl of fruit

The whole room runs the same prompt at the same time, then compares screens. This is the hook for the session and it works because the result is genuinely startling.

Activity 1
Run it together
Create an image of a bowl of fruit.

What to watch for

Near-identical results across the room — wooden bowl, apples, a banana, grapes, soft window light, three-quarter angle. Ask two people to hold their screens up next to each other.

The teaching point. With nothing to go on, the model returns the statistical middle of everything it has seen. That average carries none of your language, your judgment, or your way of doing things — and your competitors are getting the same one.

00:1818 min

House in Wisconsin

Generic first, then structured. Everyone runs the plain version, sees the postcard cliché, then rebuilds it with the full template against a house they can compare to reality. This block earns its length — let it.

Activity 2
Generic version
Create an image of a house in Wisconsin.
Structured version — from the handout
Create a realistic image of a house in Wisconsin using: house style, exterior color, roof, setting, season, time of day, yard details, and purpose. Do not include address, license plates, mailbox numbers, or identifying personal details.
The correction loop — do not start over
Keep everything exactly as it is, but change the siding from white to tan, remove the porch swing, and move the garage to the left side of the house.

What to watch for

People will try to fix a bad image by rewriting the whole prompt. Stop that early — the second and third pass should be targeted corrections, and results get visibly closer each round.

The teaching point. Specificity is not about long prompts, it is about which details you choose. Note the privacy constraint in the handout prompt — telling it what to leave out is part of the skill.

00:3610 min

Intention changes output

Same house, two reasons. This is the highest-leverage habit in the session and the one most likely to survive back at their desks.

Activity 3
Reason one
Transform the same house into a black-and-white coloring page for a child.
Reason two
Transform the same house into an architectural concept image for a remodel conversation.

The bridge to real work

Two completely different outputs, and neither required describing a drawing style. The word "for" did the work. Bridge straight to text: "summarize this meeting" versus "summarize this meeting into five bullets the yard crew can read at the start of shift." Same document, entirely different usefulness.

00:5117 min

Spreadsheets by prompt

One of the clearest demonstrations of what these tools are and are not good at. Build in stages, then collapse the whole thing into one prompt.

Activity 4
The handout prompt
Create a spreadsheet plan for tracking haul costs. Include columns, sample rows, formulas, cost per mile, cost per MBF, assumptions, and review warnings.
The one that matters most
Now give me the actual Excel formulas in the cells — do not give me the calculated numbers. Flag anything you had to assume.
The collapse
Looking back at everything we just built together, how could I have gotten all of this from a single prompt? Write me that prompt, then explain which parts of it were doing the work.

What to watch for

Have someone hand-check a subtotal against the haul cost CSV. When the tool produces numbers directly it is sometimes quietly, confidently wrong — and it never flags it.

Four problems are planted in that CSV — a zero board-feet row, a blank fuel reading, an impossible 26.5 driving hours, and 3 miles to Stevens Point. A model that hands back tidy numbers without mentioning any of them has just made your point for you. Ask the room which rows it should have caught.

AI is not a calculator. Use it to propose structure, formulas, and analysis methods. It can still choose the wrong method, use bad assumptions, or miss bad rows. Make it show its work, let Excel or another verified system perform the arithmetic, and check the result.

01:0815 min

Landowner research boundaries

Two halves. First the research shots — asking the model where Wisconsin landowner data actually lives, which nobody in the room is told in advance. Then the handout activity on the synthetic CSV, where verification and outreach language get practiced.

Activity 5
Shot 1 — find the data
How can I find all the landowners in a single Wisconsin county using free, public data? I want owner names, mailing addresses, and acreage. Tell me exactly where that data lives, who publishes it, what format it comes in, what it costs, and how current it is.
Shot 2 — narrow to land worth contacting
Now narrow it down. I only want private forested parcels big enough to be worth a conversation about timber. In Wisconsin parcel data, which field tells me a parcel is forest land, and what are the actual class codes? What acreage threshold makes sense, and why? Also explain how Managed Forest Law enrollment works and what its minimum acreage requirements are.
Shot 3 — add the forestry layer
Where do I get Managed Forest Law spatial data for Wisconsin, and how would I join it to the county parcel data? Give me the portal, the layer name, and the field I would join on. Tell me plainly what parts of MFL are published publicly and what parts are not.
Then the handout activity — synthetic CSV
Using the synthetic landowner research CSV, create a verification checklist and a careful outreach draft. Do not estimate timber value, imply Kretz inspected the property, or treat public data as verified.
Say the boundary out loud. The research shots are safe because they ask where public records live — no Kretz data leaves the room. The moment you are working with actual rows, it moves to the synthetic file. That distinction is the whole activity, and it is worth naming rather than assuming.

The model will get the shape right and some specifics wrong. Portal versions, layer names and field codes shift. Click every source it names during your dry run. If it returns something stale in the room, keep it — catching it live is the accuracy lesson in action.

Public does not mean anything goes. That records are public means you may look at them, not that any outreach is automatically appropriate. Real outreach needs agreed practices, a human review of every message, care to avoid protected or suppressed records, and a respectful way for people to decline further contact.
01:2310 min

Operational efficiency

The Decision Guide does the work here. The useful outcome is people leaving with permission to answer "not AI" — which is most of the value of having a decision guide at all.

Activity 6
The handout prompt
Using the operational efficiency CSV, classify each task as existing software first, narrow Custom GPT, automation, process cleanup, vendor system, or do not use AI yet.
Add their constraints — this is the trick
Now redo the top three with my actual constraints: who is on shift, what equipment is down, and what has a hard deadline this week. If a constraint makes your recommendation wrong, say so instead of working around it.
Run this inside the Kretz AI Decision Guide rather than a blank chat. "Existing software first" is the right answer more often than anyone expects, and a GPT that says so is more convincing than a facilitator who does.
01:338 min

Price movement scenarios

The one that can quietly undo the accuracy lesson if it is run as a demo of prediction. Short on purpose — scenarios and assumptions, never a forecast presented as fact.

Activity 7
The handout prompt
Using the synthetic lumber price movement data, identify patterns and possible leading indicators. Do not make a prediction as fact. Give scenarios, assumptions, confidence, and source-verification needs.
The deliverable worth keeping
Now turn that into a one-page monitoring template: the drivers worth tracking, where each number comes from, how often to check it, and what a meaningful change looks like versus normal noise. Use only what is in the data I gave you. If something is not in it, say so rather than filling it in.
AI cannot forecast lumber prices. Ask for next quarter's hard maple number and you get a specific, confident, well-formatted figure that is essentially invented. The rule: any market number must trace back to a source someone handed it. Market intelligence, not prophecy.

Call back to Zillow here. It is the strongest card in the Session 1 set for this activity — a company with far more pricing data than Kretz shut down home-flipping because the forecasting model could not handle volatility. That was a purpose-built model with a serious budget, not a chat prompt.

The dataset has a trap in it, and the numbers back you up. A deliberate relationship was planted in this synthetic dataset: price follows housing starts at a three-month lag — correlation 0.84 to 0.88 at lag three against 0.67 to 0.77 with no lag. Diesel, export demand, and log supply are deliberate red herrings; their correlations with price round to 0.00 at two decimal places. If the model explains why diesel drives maple, it has invented a story about numbers that provably contain none, and you can say so with a straight face.
01:4115 min

Build a tiny HTML tool

Participants pick their own problem, or follow along with the timber sale tracker. The first build is never right — that is the lesson, not a problem. The refine loop is where the value is.

Activity 8
The handout prompt
Build a single-file HTML tool to help with this problem: [describe problem]. It should have inputs, output, review warning, no external dependencies, and a print button. Ask up to five questions before coding if needed.
Refine — two changes, not twenty
Two changes only. [First change.] [Second change.] Leave everything else exactly as it is.
Open the follow-along example — the timber sale tracker. A small, working tool built from exactly two prompts, both printed on the page so the room can see the whole build. Use it if nobody has a problem of their own ready, or run it as the demo before turning them loose.

What to watch for

"Ask up to five questions before coding" is the best line in the handout. Let the model interview them — it models the whole skill back at the room without you having to narrate it.

The teaching point. Five things a tool request needs: what it does, who uses it, what goes in, what comes out, and the constraints. Anything touching customer or pricing data stays on the approved platform, and anything reaching a landowner still gets a signature.

Close and next steps

One sentence each: specificity beats volume, say who it is for, ask for formulas not answers, verify anything with a number in it, iterate instead of restarting. Then point at the package list and ask which one they want next.

The landowner sequence, in full.

Why Activity 5 opens with research prompts instead of a file, and how it connects to the prototypes already in the repo.

Two earlier example tools set this up. One was built in a single shot; the other was refined through a back-and-forth conversation. Seen side by side, they show what better prompting buys you. This activity adds the step both of them skipped: the research that finds the data before anyone writes a line of anything.

Step 3 — the research shots

New in S2

Everything in this activity is free and public

Wisconsin publishes a statewide parcel layer aggregated from all 72 counties — owner names, mailing addresses, acreage, assessed values and property class — released in annual versions and downloadable county by county at no cost, through the Department of Administration with the State Cartographer's Office. The DNR moved its forestry layers onto an open data portal alongside it. Between the two, a forester can assemble a qualified contact list for an entire county in an afternoon.

Parcel data has mailing addresses, not email addresses. What this produces is letters. If Kretz wants email delivery, those addresses have to come from existing customer records, not from public land data. Say it before anyone assumes otherwise.

It is a snapshot, not a live feed. The current training reference is Wisconsin statewide parcel release V12, built from county data collected in the first half of 2026; most tax-roll records are from 2025. Ownership changes, and county or municipal records remain the current authority. The statewide layer is not a legal survey. Public availability does not make every use appropriate: follow approved outreach practices, avoid protected or suppressed records, give recipients a respectful way to decline future contact, and require a human signature on every message.
